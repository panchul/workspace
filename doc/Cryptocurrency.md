## Smart Contracts

---

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smart_contract

---

Overview from googling:

Smart contracts are self-executing computer programs stored on a blockchain that automatically run when predetermined terms and conditions are met. By eliminating the need for central authorities, lawyers, or middlemen, they allow untrusting parties to execute secure, transparent, and irreversible transactions.

How They Work

You can think of a smart contract like a digital vending machine.

- The Code: The rules of the agreement (e.g., "if Party A sends $50, transfer ownership of the domain to them") are written into code.
- The Trigger: When a condition is satisfied and sent to the blockchain, the contract verifies the event.
- The Execution: The program automatically processes the transfer of funds, assets, or data without manual oversight.

Key Benefits

- Trustless & Transparent: Transactions are verified by the network, removing the need to rely on a third-party intermediary.
- Autonomy & Efficiency: Eliminates paperwork and speeds up settlement times since the code executes instantly once conditions are met.
- Accuracy: Reduces human error and administrative costs by relying on exact programmatic logic.
- Immutability: Once deployed to the blockchain, the contract and its transaction history are permanent and cannot be altered.

Common Use Cases

Smart contracts power the decentralized finance (DeFi) space and span multiple industries:
- Finance: Automating cryptocurrency lending, yield farming, and executing interest payments.
- Real Estate: Escrow automation where funds are securely held and released upon a clean title transfer.
- Supply Chain: Automatically verifying the location of goods and triggering payments upon delivery.
- Insurance: Triggering automatic payouts (such as flight delay insurance or crop insurance) based on real-world data feeds.

Risks & Limitations

While powerful, smart contracts come with notable challenges:
- Code Vulnerabilities: If the contract is poorly written, malicious actors can exploit bugs to steal funds.
- Irreversibility: Once code executes, mistakes or unauthorized transfers usually cannot be reversed or refunded.
- Oracle Problem: Smart contracts exist isolated on the blockchain; to interact with the real world, they rely on third-party data sources ("oracles") that introduce points of vulnerability.
- Legal Ambiguity: While code enforces the strict rules of a contract, it often lacks the nuanced flexibility and legal backing of traditional text-based contracts.

Platforms like Ethereum popularized this technology using languages like Solidity and Vyper, and many other blockchains now support smart contract capabilities.
